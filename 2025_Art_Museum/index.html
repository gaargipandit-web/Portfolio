Project Brief

The 2025 TSA Architectural Design challenge tasked participants with designing a museum within a two-acre site while incorporating exhibition galleries, classrooms, storage areas, offices, restrooms, a theater, food services, and all required accessibility and safety features.

To address this challenge, I developed a concept inspired by the connected Julia set, a fractal known for its self-similar branching geometry. Fractal forms provide a balance of symmetry, complexity, and modularity, creating a layout that avoids the rigid, block-like appearance of conventional buildings. The resulting design features sweeping galleries connected by branching corridors that open into larger exhibition spaces, creating a sense of discovery while efficiently utilizing the available site.

A centrally located entrance allows visitors to navigate the museum organically, dispersing crowds and encouraging exploration. Rather than directing guests through a predetermined path, the layout supports a more personal and reflective experience with the artwork.

Beyond its functional role, the project explores the relationship between art and mathematics. Fractal geometry demonstrates how simple mathematical rules can generate visually rich and aesthetically engaging forms. By transforming a mathematical object into an architectural space, the museum itself becomes part of the exhibit—blurring the boundary between artistic expression and mathematical design.

The final proposal satisfied all TSA program requirements, incorporated sustainable design principles, and complied with Florida accessibility and life-safety standards. The project was recognized as a TSA Architectural Design National Finalist, placing among the top ten entries in the United States.

30,000+ sq ft of Exhibition Space

Over 32,000 square feet of programmed space distributed across interconnected galleries and support facilities.

Three-Level Museum Experience

Multiple floors dedicated to galleries, educational spaces, offices, storage, and an auditorium.

Crowd-Dispersing Circulation

Branching pathways encourage exploration while preventing bottlenecks in high-traffic areas.

Sustainable Building Systems

Incorporates green roofs, high-performance glazing, and energy-conscious environmental controls.

Code-Driven Design Process

Developed using real site constraints, accessibility requirements, parking calculations, plumbing systems, HVAC planning, and life-safety considerations.

Universal Accessibility

Barrier-free circulation with ramps, elevators, and accessible pathways integrated into the architectural form rather than added as afterthoughts.

Indoor–Outdoor Integration

Green roofs, terraces, and outdoor gathering spaces blur the boundary between architecture and landscape while creating opportunities for relaxation and informal exhibitions.

Modular Spatial Organization

Repeating cylindrical modules create a flexible framework that can accommodate galleries, educational spaces, workshops, cafés, and future expansion without disrupting the overall composition.

Daylighting Strategy

Extensive glazing and skylights maximize natural light while enhancing visual connections between spaces.
